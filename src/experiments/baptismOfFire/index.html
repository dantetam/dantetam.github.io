The Witcher (Baptism of Fire ) Review  


Dante Tam, 2/15/2018


Baptism of Fire builds up the Witcher's high fantasy world, which develops the characters into tragic heroes through tramuatic and cathartic trials. The 'baptism' represents a violent fire that purges the characters of their evils and moral flaws, which strengthens them against their future adversity.


Sapkowski unites the unique cast of characters in a complex, intertwined set of narratives. Even Geralt's group itself realizes its own humorous composition. Yet, all the characters are linked together by their own tragic trials and tribulations, and their ultimate redemption in the face of evil and violence — the baptism of fire.

1. (Spoiler.) In The Time of Contempt, Geralt visits the isle of Thanedd, a magical island that was once an elven ruin. The sorcerers bring the island to life with illusion magic, which fills in the cracks of the decrepit and ruined isle, and colors its brick walls and bridges. This magical barrier also blocks violent magic. The meeting of magicians and sorceresses is built on artificial protocols, polite facades, and strict (unspoken) etiquette — all of which masks a deeper political intrigue. The high magical illusion of Thanedd fades away to leave behind the ugly physical ruins of the isle. The great facade breaks down to reveal primitive humans and their behaviors: excessive eating and drinking; telling vulgar stories around ruins and fires; and violence in the place of politics. ↩
