Affirmative Action in 2020: An Editorial Deep Dive into the University of California


Dante Tam, 10/7/2020


The passage of Proposition 16 in California would enable affirmative action in public state institutions. It attempts to overturn Prop 209, which California voters passed in 1996 to ban the consideration of race. These measures greatly affect the University of California system, which must choose its criteria for ranking candidates.


We will discuss affirmative action as the consideration of race in the enrollment process at the UC system. 'Underrepresented minority' (URM), is an engineered term unique to the UC system and refers to any non-White ethnicity that has less share in enrollment than its share of the state's population. Prop 209 greatly decreased African American and Latino admissions. For both UC Berkeley and UCLA, admit rates dropped from 7% in 1997 to 3% in 1998, the first year of Prop 209's enforcement.1 URM graduation rates increased greatly in the immediate aftermath of Prop 209. 2 This sudden change over a year reveals the massive scope of discrimination in admissions.


Race in admissions leads to uncomfortable situations in which more qualified non-URM applicants will be discarded in favor of URM applicants. Affirmative action supporters call for race to be considered alongside high school grades, AP courses, and extracurricular activities.
