Fallout as a Social Critique  PDF


Dante Tam, 10/27/2017


The video game series Fallout deeply explores the paranoid consumerism of the Cold War era,
through fun, immersive game mechanics, and a rich lore illustrating a horrific, life-like fantasy.


In the Fallout universe, the world has not culturally advanced beyond the 1950s, with vacuum tube computers,

lack of a counterculture movement, etc. A Cold War paranoia continues from the end of WWII, all the way to the late 21st century,

where large fallout shelters, Vaults, have been built across America.

Thermonuclear war ensues, and the world wakes up to a new reality of irradiated animals, savage tribespeople,
and the breakdown of civilization and its replacement with quasi-legitimate states and communities.


On the surface, Fallout quite obviously portrays and satirizes historical Cold War era culture.
In the commonness and ubiquity of "pre-War" goods like Nuka-Cola and "Cram" (derived from Spam),
this world establishes a parallel to our own — a world of brands and factory made products.
There are many types of drugs, cocktails, and stimulants across the Wasteland, which represent
the growing narcotics and substance abuse health issues in the past and in the present-day US.
Vault "culture" is particularly strong. The Vaults are distinct only in their undying, monolithic structure.
They are identical down to the design, manufacturer, Vault attire (Vault jumpsuits) — save for the vault number.
The premise is still the same in each numbered vault: to live a calm, sterile life, away from the madness of the world.
